Most English plurals are formed with -s or -es too. There is the occasional mutation in final consonant (wolf -> wolves), just as in French (cheval -> chevaux). The apostrophe+s plural happens some of the time, but not as often as some writers might think.

ObTopic: "Cars", "engines", "tracks", "opponents", and "cops" have no apostrophe. Nor do "methods", "textures", "meshes", "maps", and "waves".

other examples of "'s" cases (the so called "possessive"): "a sniper's rifle" (his rifle), "i am going to the doctor's" (his office) and "i'm over at Ronny's" (his house), as well as "the NDS' game library rocks!"

:^)

as you can see, if the last letter is an 's', you write the apostroph and leave out the s, but when you say it it sounds like "en dee esses" (or "?n d? ?ss?s" [that might me totally *wrong*], if you like ;^D ).
